Based on the two live sites, the strategic issue is clear: OWL’s current website is still mostly a corporate/investor-facing wrapper around FWBM, with navigation centered on Home, FWBM, Vision & Technology, Founders, Investors, and Contact. FWBM, by contrast, already behaves like a product platform: it has TR/EN switching, login, weekly-updated dashboards, Monet as an AI analysis assistant, external data integrations, and a free-trial CTA. In practical terms, the new build should position OWL as a portfolio company with multiple products, rather than as a single-product brochure. ([owlintelligence.co.uk][1])

My recommendation is a three-layer structure. Layer one is OWL Intelligence as the umbrella brand: corporate credibility, founders, governance, UK/Türkiye presence, technology vision, partnerships, and investor-grade trust signals. Layer two is the product portfolio: FWBM and FuzzyOwl as distinct commercial products with separate narratives, demos, pricing logic, and sector use cases. Layer three is the application layer: interactive tools, gated demos, and a single OWL agent that routes users toward the right product, dataset, or commercial path. This fits your current market position because the existing sites already signal financial intelligence, AI, live dashboards, and productization, but they do not yet surface a true portfolio architecture. ([owlintelligence.co.uk][1])

I would structure the website like this. The homepage should present OWL as “AI-powered intelligence products grounded in authentic field data,” then immediately split traffic into FWBM, FuzzyOwl, Solutions, Insights, and Talk to an Agent. FWBM should have its own product page with modules, methodology, sample dashboard views, use cases, trial/demo flow, and a clear bridge to the live product. FuzzyOwl should have its own product page with a simpler explanation of FCM, what the package includes, what deliverables clients receive, how AI and visualization strengthen the workflow, and a guided mini-demo. Then add a Solutions layer for Banks & Insurers, FMCG/Retail/Telecom, Public Institutions, Investors/ESG, and Research & Strategy Teams, because those sectors are already implicit in your current positioning. ([owlintelligence.co.uk][1])

The information architecture should be bilingual from day one, not “English site plus partly translated product pages.” I would use mirrored route logic such as `/en/...` and `/tr/...`, with a shared design system and content model underneath. Next.js currently supports App Router-based internationalization and localized routing, which makes this approach operationally clean. ([Next.js][2])

For the domain model, I would not collapse everything into one domain. Keep `owlintelligence.co.uk` as the master brand and commercial portfolio site. Keep `fwbm.com.tr` as the dedicated product/application domain for FWBM, but redesign it so it visually and structurally feels like part of the OWL ecosystem. Launch FuzzyOwl either as a clean product path under the master domain first, such as `owlintelligence.co.uk/fuzzyowl`, or later as its own domain once demand is proven. That gives you one strong parent brand, while preserving product-level SEO and conversion funnels. This also reflects the fact that FWBM already has more application-like behavior than the current OWL site. ([fwbm.com.tr][3])

On stack, I would use Next.js App Router with TypeScript as the core front end. Today’s official Next.js guidance emphasizes App Router, layouts, file-system routing, route groups, dynamic routes, and internationalization support, all of which fit a bilingual portfolio site with separate marketing, product, and app-like sections. It is also a good fit for a future split between public pages and more dynamic authenticated experiences. ([Next.js][4])

For content, I would use a headless CMS rather than hardcoding all bilingual marketing pages. Sanity is a strong fit here because its Content Lake is structured and queryable, and its localization model is designed for multilingual content workflows. That matters for OWL because you will likely manage product pages, founder profiles, use cases, insight articles, reports, and sector-specific landing pages in both Turkish and English. ([Sanity.io][5])

For backend and data services, Supabase is the pragmatic option. Its official docs show a full Postgres database, Auth, Storage, and Edge Functions in one stack. It also has official guidance for automatic embeddings and vector-like semantic retrieval patterns, which is useful for powering an agent over your product docs, methods, case studies, and FAQs without building a separate retrieval layer too early. ([Supabase][6])

For deployment, I would use Vercel. Its current documentation is explicit about preview deployments, routing middleware, and edge-oriented request handling. That gives you a clean setup for bilingual redirects, campaign landing pages, gated previews, and fast iteration with design or messaging experiments. ([Vercel][7])

For the AI layer, I would build on the OpenAI Responses API plus the Agents SDK. OpenAI’s current docs position Responses as the API surface that can call built-in tools and custom tools, while the Agents SDK adds agent definitions, tools, streaming, handoffs, and traces. In other words, it is suitable for a public-facing commercial agent if you keep the tool surface controlled and the retrieval corpus curated. ([OpenAI Platform][8])

For analytics and controlled rollout, I would add PostHog. Its current docs cover product analytics, feature flags, experiments, and session replay. That is useful because your first version of the agent, the FuzzyOwl demo, and the FWBM conversion paths should all be tested iteratively instead of being treated as fixed assets. ([PostHog][9])

On agents, I would not launch several unrelated chatbots. I would launch one orchestrated OWL Agent with specialized internal modes. Publicly, that agent should do four jobs. First, qualify the visitor: sector, role, geography, problem, and urgency. Second, route them to the right product: FWBM, FuzzyOwl, or a bespoke consulting conversation. Third, answer method and product questions using a curated retrieval base. Fourth, convert: trial, demo, meeting, or contact form. Internally, the same orchestration layer can later branch to a Proposal Copilot, an Onboarding Copilot, and a Client Support Copilot. This is the cleanest commercial model because it keeps the front-end interaction simple while preserving agent specialization behind the scenes.

For website tools, I would prioritize five. First, a “Which OWL product fits your need?” decision tool. Second, a FWBM public demo explorer with safe sample indicators, trend cards, and a few interactive cuts. Third, a FuzzyOwl mini playground that shows how a fuzzy cognitive map is built from concepts, relationships, and scenario simulations. Fourth, a methodology explorer that turns your research rigor into a commercial differentiator. Fifth, a report and insights library with filters by sector, topic, and language. These are more commercially useful than decorative widgets because they convert expertise into tangible product experience.

The minimum viable sitemap I would recommend is this: Home, Products, FWBM, FuzzyOwl, Solutions, Technology & AI, Insights, About OWL, Founders, Investors & Partners, Contact, and Agent. Under the hood, the public route groups should be separated from gated app routes. If later needed, Next.js also supports route groups and multi-zone patterns for splitting large experiences into smaller applications under one domain structure. ([Next.js][10])

A practical build sequence would be: first, redesign the master OWL site and unify brand language across English and Turkish. Second, create the two product pages and a shared design system. Third, implement the OWL Agent with retrieval over curated product and methodology content. Fourth, launch the interactive FWBM and FuzzyOwl demos. Fifth, add CRM handoff, analytics, experiments, and gated partner/client spaces. This reduces execution risk and lets you commercialize quickly without overengineering phase one.

My recommendation in one line is this: build OWL as a bilingual AI-intelligence portfolio platform, not as a corporate brochure; keep FWBM and FuzzyOwl as distinct product businesses within that platform; and deploy one commercial agent that qualifies, routes, educates, and converts.

The strongest stack for that is: Next.js + TypeScript + Tailwind/shadcn for the front end, Sanity for bilingual content operations, Supabase for auth/data/storage/retrieval, OpenAI Responses API + Agents SDK for the agent layer, Vercel for deployment, and PostHog for analytics and experimentation. ([Next.js][2])

The next concrete step should be a proper sitemap and page-by-page content blueprint for OWL, FWBM, and FuzzyOwl.
